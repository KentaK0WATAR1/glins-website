About GLINS

What is GLINS?

The GLINS (Global Logistics Intermodal Network Simulation) model was developed to help policymakers and practitioners understand and evaluate international logistics flows in an increasingly interconnected world. As maritime container transport has globalized, it has become essential to analyze not just shipping routes but also the wider hinterland networks that connect ports to inland destinations.

Since its inception, GLINS has evolved through applications in diverse regions and contexts, responding to specific policy needs and incorporating new features along the way. Initially focused on maritime networks alone, it has progressively expanded to include hinterland congestion, land freight, air cargo, and even non-containerized cargo. It has also introduced multi-stage allocation to better represent complex logistics decisions.

Over the past decade, GLINS has been applied in Central America, South and Southeast Asia, Central and East Africa, Eurasia, Japan, the Pacific Islands, and the Arctic region. These applications have addressed challenges such as prioritizing infrastructure investments, evaluating policy options, optimizing routes under capacity and congestion constraints, and assessing the viability of new shipping routes like the Arctic sea route, where seasonal conditions significantly affect costs and accessibility.

Today, GLINS provides a robust and practical framework for analyzing international logistics flows—including emerging routes such as Arctic shipping—and supporting evidence-based decisions. Its ongoing development continues to reflect both the complexities of global trade and the practical needs of the stakeholders who shape it.
